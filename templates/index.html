Byte 6 - Social Networks & Big Data

Choosing the Social Networks portion of this Byte, I took a look at two of the provided datasets, for Facebook and Amazon, while attempting to work with a third outside dataset which I will discuss below.

Facebook & Amazon

I selected the Facebook common friends data and Amazon co-purchases datasets to work with, as they turned out to be the two I could most readily work with on my laptop machine. The size of these data sets made iteration challenging, but I found a good compormise between size and performance with these two sets.

Data exploration and visualization

Building off of the provided code, I worked to get the networks to map and visualize in an inteligible and insightful manner. This presented a number of challenges with the suggested visualization method, the force directed graph. The data was so large that the dynamic nature of the graph became not only moot, but even a hiderence to the experience. I was unable to manipulate the graphs, once rendered, in a performant way.

I ran a number of different k-core algorithm configurations on the two datasets, in order to explore how to consolidate some of the datapoints into neighborhoods that would both visualize as well as perform better. The results, charted, for these explorations were as follow:

I found that in the Facebook dataset, the least common components came back as "1" everytime. This seemed sensible enough, as finding users who share at least one person in common on a large social network does not seem too challenging, with all the data at your dispsal.

With the Amazon set, I found much more varied results as I changed the value of K in the algorithm. I found ranges from 1,700 all the way to as few as 2. This I suppose is not all that challenging, as there would likely be much more variety, and repetition, in co-purchase history. As I increased K, reaching for edges that shared at least K nodes in common, I found that number went further down. I likened that to finding individual purchasers who had 6 or more purchases in common. Even still, these results plotted very densly.

Both of these datasets plotted along with according to the Power Law, as shown below:

I spent a lot of time modifying the values for the various force diagram plot settings, changing the gravity and the friction so as to be able to contain more of the visualization within the viewport. This worked out extremely well with the Facebook data set, and less pleasingly with the Amazon set. It is clear that further work to both improve the plot mechanics, as well as to better represent the scale of the data would be necessary if these graphs were to ever be used to really explore and learn from.

The visualizations above are shown in increasing order of values for K in my k-core algorithms. As I increased this value, I found the noise cleared up, and the graphs became easier to work with. Amazon, while less obvious, definitely rendered and "settled" faster as the value of K increased. The density was still overwhelming.
